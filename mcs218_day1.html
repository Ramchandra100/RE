🔁 1. Data Transmission Basics & Modes

Simplex · Half-Duplex · Full-Duplex — the direction of data flow

📖 Explain

Data communication requires a protocol and a channel. Data flows in three fundamental modes:

  • Simplex Mode: One-way communication only. A device is either transmitter or receiver.
  • Half-Duplex Mode: Both devices can transmit and receive, but not simultaneously. Channel flips direction.
  • Full-Duplex Mode: Simultaneous bidirectional transmission. Channel capacity is shared.
📌 Examples:
🎙️ Simplex: FM Radio / Keyboard → computer.
📻 Half-Duplex: Walkie-talkie (push-to-talk).
☎️ Full-Duplex: Telephone call, modern Ethernet.

📈 2. Analog vs. Digital Signals

Continuous waves vs discrete levels — noise resilience & representation

📖 Explain

Analog signals are continuous time-varying waves (infinite values). Prone to noise degradation.
Digital signals use discrete voltage levels (0/1). Highly immune to noise, used by computers.

🕰️ Analog: traditional clock, human voice.
⌚ Digital: digital watch, CPU processing binary data.

Because digital signals only need to distinguish between a limited set of values, they are preferred in modern networking.

🔀 3. Multiplexing: FDM & TDM

Sharing a single channel — Frequency Division & Time Division techniques

📖 Explain

Multiplexing combines multiple signals into one for efficient channel usage. MUX at sender, DEMUX at receiver.

  • FDM (Frequency Division Multiplexing): Divides the frequency spectrum into separate bands. Each user gets a dedicated frequency. Uses guard bands to prevent interference. Example: Radio/TV broadcasting.
  • TDM (Time Division Multiplexing): Divides time into slots. Each user gets full bandwidth but only for a short time slice. Example: Multiple terminals sharing a single line.
📡 FDM: Different radio stations (98.3 MHz, 93.5 MHz) transmit simultaneously.
🎤 TDM: People sharing a microphone — each speaks in turn.

📝 Check Your Progress — Exam-Style Q&A

Q1. What is multiplexing? Why is it needed in data communication?
Answer: Multiplexing is the process of combining multiple data streams into a single composite signal for transmission over a shared channel. It is needed because providing dedicated links for every pair of devices is expensive and inefficient; multiplexing maximizes channel utilization and reduces infrastructure costs.
Q2. Explain the difference between a Multiplexer and a Demultiplexer.
Answer: A Multiplexer (MUX) combines n input signals into one output at the sender side. A Demultiplexer (DEMUX) receives the composite signal and separates it back into n original signals at the receiver side.
Q3. Describe Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM).
Answer: FDM is an analog technique where the channel’s total bandwidth is divided into non-overlapping frequency bands, each assigned to a different user. All users transmit simultaneously on their dedicated frequency.
Q4. What is the purpose of "guard bands" in FDM?
Answer: Guard bands are unused frequency gaps placed between adjacent channels to prevent interference (cross-talk) caused by imperfect filters, ensuring signals remain isolated.
Q5. Briefly explain Time Division Multiplexing (TDM) and how it differs from FDM.
Answer: TDM divides time into slots; each user gets the full bandwidth for a fixed time interval in a round-robin manner. Difference: FDM shares the channel by dividing frequency (simultaneous transmission), while TDM shares by dividing time (sequential transmission).
