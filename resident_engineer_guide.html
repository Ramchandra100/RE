Part 1: Cables & Connectors (The Physical Layer)

As a Resident Engineer, your first instinct when something is broken should always be to check the physical connection.

Ethernet Cable (RJ45)

The lifeline of the network. Connects desktops, printers, and APs to the internet.

The Golden Rule: Listen for a distinct “CLICK”. If the plastic clip breaks, the cable slips → random disconnections.

The Lights: Green/amber blinking LED near port = live connection. No light = no network.

PRO TIP Always replace broken clip patch cords — never re-use.

Display: VGA vs HDMI

VGA (Video Graphics Array) – Older, blue, 15-pin. Video only, no audio. Two screws: tighten them. Loose VGA = pink/yellow tint (color pins lose contact).

HDMI (High-Definition Multimedia Interface) – Modern standard. Carries high-quality video + audio in a single flat connector.

Field Fix: Pink screen on projector? Reseat VGA and screw both sides tight.
Part 2: The Network Rack (The Brains of the Building)

Inside the server room or telecom closet: two main pieces that work together.

Patch Panel
Passive · No power · No lights
Wall cables terminate here
⟷ Patch Cords
Short, cheap, replaceable
Network Switch
Active · Needs power · Blinking LEDs
Provides actual network & internet

Patch Panel Explained

Fragile in-wall cables (running from wall sockets) end safely at the back of the panel. It's a passive termination point.

Network Switch

Uses short patch cords to connect patch panel ports to switch ports. If a clip breaks on a patch cord → toss it (₹50 cable). Protects expensive in-wall infrastructure.

Design wisdom: Never plug building’s wall cables directly into switch. Patch panel saves the day.

Quick Quiz: Level 1

Test your foundation. Click any question to reveal the answer.

Q1: A user complains their internet keeps disconnecting when they bump their desk. What is the very first physical thing you should check?

Answer: Check the RJ45 Ethernet cable to see if the small plastic retention clip is broken. If it is broken, the cable is loose.

Q2: You plug an Ethernet cable into a PC, but the small LED lights next to the port are completely dark. What does this mean?

Answer: It means there is no physical connection to the network switch. The cable is either unplugged at the wall/switch, the cable is dead, or the switch port is turned off.

Q3: An instructor is using an older projector with a VGA cable, and the screen looks entirely pink. How do you fix it?

Answer: Push the VGA cable firmly into the port and tighten the two screws on the sides. A pink/yellow tint means the pins are loose.

Q4: Why do we use a Patch Panel instead of plugging the long cables from the office walls directly into the Network Switch?

Answer: To protect the expensive wall cables. If we plugged them directly into the switch and the plastic clip broke, we would have to pull a whole new cable through the building's ceiling.

Q5: Which display cable carries both video and audio in a single connection?

Answer: HDMI. (VGA is video only).
